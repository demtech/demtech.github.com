About DemTech

Computers are gradually replacing the manual parts of the electoral process. For instance, information technology is used in many countries for registering voters, maintaining the electoral roll, casting votes, tallying results and computing seat assignments to parties in parliament. Politicians and administrators often advocate the use of technology with the aim to make the electoral process more efficient, informative and economical.

However, software systems are complex, prone to programming error, and are vulnerable to malicious attacks. If not addressed early, these problems will have a negative effect on the very foundations of democracy.


The Demtech Vision

DemTech provides answers to the question of how to modernize the democratic process without jeopardizing the fundamental principles on which democracies are built. To do so, DemTech focuses on the voting technology as a whole, concentrating not only on technology and security, but also on questions related to organization, trust, and social choice. To ensure that voters' trust that their votes have been cast and counted correctly, we also focus on the social, political and cultural implications inherent in the digital transformation of the democratic process.

Independent research

DemTech aims to provide decision makers important and invaluable insights into how voting technology can—or cannot—benefit the electoral process. It is important to realize that we do not sell any technology or any technical solution. In fact, all our results and findings are public so that anybody—the government, the public, and also the industry—can benefit from our research. In that way we hope to avoid conflict of interest and to increase our trustworthiness.

The DemTech mission

Since the year 2000, all but eleven countries have held national elections, and many of these countries used, or at least experimented with, information technology to assist the electoral process. Some held binding internet elections; others decided to continue to conduct elections the traditional way. The experiences collected during these elections, in particular, advantages, disadvantages, requirements, evaluations and risk analyses, are of interests to the international community. DemTech strives to help design, evaluate, learn from, and evolve these experiments and contribute with insights regarding the difficult technological questions related to the secrecy, security, and integrity of the election in the greater societal context. DemTech brings together internationally leading experts on voting technology and elections. Our international research staff collaborates, communicates, and consults with governments, administrations, industries and academics worldwide.

Showcase: The Danish Project

The first DemTech research project started July 2011 and runs until June 2016. As part of the project we develop a novel software design principle called "Trust by Design" which gives developers a means by which to develop trustworthy technologies that people rely on from the traditional voting process. The hypothesis of this research project is the following: Is it possible to modernize the electoral process while balancing the trust of the people on the trustworthiness of the deployed technology?

The DemTech project is based at the IT University of Copenhagen, has staff at the Technical University of Denmark, and has partnered with research institutions in Luxembourg and Switzerland and local Danish governments. DemTech's external advisory board monitors DemTech's research activities. Furthermore, DemTech collaborates with the Danish Ministry for Economic and Interior Affairs on questions related to the modernization of the Danish electoral process.

Activities

DemTech is involved in a number of projects giving counsel and making contributions on a neutral and scientific basis. Both to the scientific field and the public decision process and debate. The cases displayed below are examples on processes in which DemTech has been involved in some extend.

Danish law proposal on e-elections

In 2012 the Danish parliament treated a law proposal to start trials on electronic voting. The proposal was much debated in the media and DemTech was cited many places in Denmark (Version2, Jyllands-Posten, Information, Berlingske).

On a political level DemTech engaged in a public collaboration with the ministry for economics and the interior to provide expert advise to the decision makers and officials. Additionally DemTech submitted an official response to the law (see below).

In the process DemTech took care to only convey knowledge based in research so the decision makers could take the most informed decision possible.
